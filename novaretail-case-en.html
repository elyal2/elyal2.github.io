02 · The Conflict

Two months to Black Friday. An ultimatum.

Manuel, the CTO, is worried. Last year the system crashed three times during peak traffic. Management no longer accepts patches.

The CEO to Manuel: "I don't want more people manually entering data. I want the technology to work on its own. You have €200,000 and until launch to prove it."
Manuel at the board meeting: "The Customer Service department had to hire 40 temps for tasks my 8-year-old could do if he had access to all four screens at once."

The problem is not about people. It's about architecture. Four systems that don't share data in real time, five logistics portals with different APIs, and a stock synchronization process that depends on a CSV downloaded once a day.

Manuel must decide: what to solve first, with what technology, and how to avoid creating a bigger problem than the one he's trying to fix.

03 · The Three Pain Points

Not all problems deserve the same solution

Before proposing any technology, the team needs to thoroughly understand the three problems. Read them carefully — you'll use them across all four tasks.

A
High frequency · Very high cumulative impact

The Customer Service "Labyrinth"

60% of tickets are variants of the same question: "Where is my order and why does it say it's out for delivery if I haven't received anything?"

Current process
The agent opens the ticket → opens the carrier portal → copies the tracking code → pastes it → detects the error → enters the ERP → verifies the address → drafts and sends the response to the customer.
The real cost
8 minutes per ticket. With 400 daily tickets during Black Friday, that amounts to 53 hours of human work every day handling a single type of query.
B
Medium frequency · High direct economic impact

The Stock "Chaos"

When a product runs out on the web but the ERP records 2 available units (due to desynchronization), the system keeps selling. The result: cancellations, costly returns, and marketplace penalties that damage product positioning.

Current process
An intern downloads a CSV from SAP once a day and manually uploads it to Shopify. The desynchronization window can be up to 23 hours.
The real cost
Each overselling incident generates: return cost + reverse logistics + marketplace penalty + dissatisfied customer. During traffic spikes like Black Friday, this process is a machine for losing money and reputation.
C
Low frequency · Critical impact · Reputational risk

IT "Fires"

Each traffic spike saturates the database. The engineering team abandons product development to do emergency plumbing work: cleaning logs, restarting servers, manually adjusting the load balancer.

Current process
An on-call engineer receives an alert — frequently at 3 AM —, connects, manually diagnoses the problem, and applies the fix. The average resolution time is 47 minutes.
The real cost
Each hour of downtime during Black Friday means hundreds of thousands of euros in lost sales. Moreover, the most valuable technical team burns their time on operational work instead of building product.
04 · Teamwork

Four tasks to build NovaRetail's strategy

Work in groups. Each group must complete all four tasks and be prepared to defend their decisions to the class. There is no single correct answer, but there are better-supported decisions than others.

01
Task 1

Classify to avoid mistakes: Agent, automation, or nothing?

⏱ 20 minutes

Before writing a single line of code or hiring a vendor, you need to know what type of solution each problem deserves. Use the 3R Test and the Agentic Decision Matrix we covered in class to classify problems A, B, and C.

Reminder — The 3R Test: a process needs agentic capability if it answers "yes" to two or more of these questions: Does the decision change depending on context (Reasoning)? Does it require accessing three or more different systems with transformation between them (Relationship with tools)? Must it work even if the input format changes without notice (Resilience)?

  • Apply the 3R Test to each of the three problems. Which ones cross the agentic threshold and which don't?
  • Place the three problems on the Impact vs. Frequency Matrix. Which zone does each occupy: classic automation, agent sweet spot, human copilot, or simply ignore?
  • With a budget of €200,000 and only four weeks until Black Friday, which problem do you tackle first? Justify your choice with ROI criteria, not just technical complexity.
  • For which of the three problems would it be a mistake to use an LLM? What simpler solution would solve the problem equally well or better?
📋
Deliverable
Table with each problem's classification (3R test + matrix zone) and reasoned priority order.
02
Task 2

Design the agent: Customer Service and IT Self-remediation

⏱ 25 minutes

For the two problems you've identified as agentic candidates, design the high-level workflow. This is not about technical architecture — it's about defining what the agent decides, how far its autonomy extends, and when it needs a human.

Part A — The Customer Service Agent:

  • Input: Through which channel does the request arrive? Does the channel matter for how the agent reasons?
  • Tools: List the APIs the agent needs to read (tracking, ERP, customer history) and the ones it needs to write to (respond in Zendesk, notify the logistics team, update the ticket).
  • Decision tree: What does the agent do if the carrier responds "Incorrect Address"? What if the customer has already opened three incidents this month? What if the order exceeds €500?
  • Autonomy limit: For which decisions must the agent request human approval before acting? What is the maximum tolerable "blast radius" if the agent makes a mistake?

Part B — The IT Self-Remediation Agent:

  • Define which operations the agent can execute autonomously and which require explicit human approval. Why that line and not another?
  • What information should the notification to the team include when the agent escalates a problem, so the human can decide within 30 seconds?
  • If the agent applies an incorrect fix, how do you ensure you can revert to the previous state? Does the system design change if rollback is impossible?
🗺️
Deliverable
Flowchart (paper or whiteboard) for each agent + permissions table (what it can / cannot do without approval).

05 · The Unexpected Twist

The DPO Hits the Brakes

Just before deployment, NovaRetail's Data Protection Officer stops the project. She doesn't deny the technology. She raises three objections that any executive must be able to answer before putting an agent or ML model into production in Europe.

Objection 01 · Data Sovereignty

Where do our customer data live when the agent processes them?

The agents and models call LLMs and APIs whose servers are in the U.S. The DPO asks: "How do we ensure that our Spanish customers' data are not used to train third-party models in California? Have we conducted the required DPIA?" The GDPR requires explicit legal basis for any international transfer of personal data.

Objection 02 · Automated Decisions · Art. 22 GDPR

The agent can deny a refund. Does it have the right to do so without human review?

The Customer Service agent has the autonomy to classify a return as "potential fraud" via ML and automatically block the refund. Art. 22 of the GDPR establishes that every European citizen has the right not to be subject to exclusively automated decisions that produce legal effects or significantly affect them. The DPO demands a visible human appeal mechanism for every automated resolution.

Objection 03 · Algorithmic Bias · AI Act Art. 6

The forecasting model is excluding certain postal codes. Is that discrimination?

The demand forecasting model — the one you proposed in Task 3 — is suggesting reducing the stock allocated to certain postal codes because "they historically buy less". The DPO warns: historical data encode historical biases. Systematically disadvantaging customers by their postal code may constitute algorithmic discrimination under the AI Act if the model influences access to products or pricing. This system would already fall into the high-risk category under Article 6.

04
Task 4

Respond to the DPO as if you were the CEO

⏱ 25 minutes · Role-play + plenary debate

The CEO has asked you to prepare the answers to the three objections before the executive committee. You can't block the project, but you can't ignore the regulatory risk either. You need to be able to say "yes, under these conditions."

  • What solution do you propose for data sovereignty? Models with EU data residency, on-premise processing, data processing contracts with providers? What cost and timeline does each option imply?
  • Design the human appeal workflow for automated refund decisions. Who receives the appeal? Within what timeframe must it be resolved? How does the agent document its reasoning so the human reviewer can understand it without technical knowledge?
  • The forecasting model you proposed in Task 3 has a risk of geographic bias. How do you detect it before it causes harm? What metrics do you monitor? Who in the organization is responsible for continuously auditing it?
  • If the project is delayed three months due to regulatory compliance work, how do you present that delay to the CEO — as a cost or as an investment?
🎙️
Final plenary
Each group presents their position in 2 minutes. The facilitator acts as DPO and can object. The debate will be won by the group that best combines implementation speed, technical feasibility, and regulatory risk management.
